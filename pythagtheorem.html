The Pythagorean Theorem: Explained


The Pythagorean theorem states that in a right-angled triangle, the square of the hypotenuse (the side opposite the right angle, c) is equal to the sum of the squares of the other two sides (a and b), represented as a² + b² = c².


Here's a breakdown:

Identify a right-angled triangle: Make sure the triangle you are working with has a 90-degree angle.

Label the sides:

Hypotenuse (c): The longest side, opposite the right angle.

Other two sides (a and b): The sides that form the right angle.

Apply the formula: a² + b² = c².

If finding the hypotenuse (c): Square sides a and b, add those squares, and then take the square root of the sum to find c.

If finding a side (a or b): Square the other two sides, subtract the square of the side you know from the square of the hypotenuse, and then take the square root of the result.

Example: If a = 3 and b = 4, then c² = 3² + 4² = 9 + 16 = 25, and c = √25 = 5.

In Summary: The Pythagorean Theorem allows you to find a missing side length of a right triangle using the relationship of the squares of its sides: a² + b² = c².
